The church of San Carlo Borromeo (also known as San Carlo al Porto ) in Via del Porto has a very troubled architectural history, marked by continuous evolutions and almost total destruction during the Second World War.

1. The origins: From the "Crocetta" to the seventeenth century

Initially, in that place there stood a Marian image called Santa Maria del Paradiso , placed next to a cross (hence the ancient name of the area, "la Crocetta").

  • 1466: The Confraternity of Santa Maria del Paradiso was founded and built its first church.

  • 1612-1619: The Confraternity changes its name in honor of Saint Charles Borromeo. In 1619, the foundation stone of a new church, designed by the architect Nicol√≤ Barelli , is laid . This building was consecrated in 1644.

2. The eighteenth-century expansion

During the 18th century, the church underwent major works to adapt to the Baroque taste and the growing needs of the brotherhood:

  • 1720-1728: The side chapels dedicated to St. Anthony and the Blessed Virgin are added.

  • 1746: The structures were raised and equipped with vaults, giving the church a majestic appearance that it would maintain until the 20th century.

3. Destruction and modern reconstruction

The most dramatic moment in the building's history was January 29, 1944. A violent Allied bombing raid hit the port area and the nearby station hard, almost razing the church and the archiepiscopal seminary to the ground.

  • The "Miracle" of the Oratory: While the church was almost entirely destroyed, the Oratory of San Carlo (built in 1667 on the upper floor and adjacent to the church) remained incredibly intact. This event was considered miraculous by the faithful, who attributed it to the intercession of Saint Anthony of Padua (who lived in a nearby convent).

  • The current building: The church we see today is a post-war reconstruction (completed in the 1950s based on a design by Alfredo Cosentino ). It is a modern building, a "parallelepiped" with clean lines and covered in marble, which contrasts sharply with the Baroque style of the surviving oratory next door.

In short: what to see today

Today the complex is an architectural "hybrid":

  1. The New Church: Functional, modern, typical of post-war architecture.

The Oratory (17th century): Accessible through the church, it is a perfectly preserved baroque jewel, with a richly frescoed vault depicting St. Charles in glory.
