It's a classic example of how Bologna's history remains embedded in its walls, even in alleys off the beaten track.

The building housing the newsstand in the photo is a veritable palimpsest of Bolognese history. Here are some interesting facts about the Compagnia dell'Avesella and the context of that wall:

Avesella Company

This brotherhood was one of the most active in the neighborhood. Its name derives from the Avesa stream (or Avesella canal ), which once flowed openly through this very area.

  • Charitable purpose: The Company managed the Hospital and Oratory of Santa Maria della Pioggia, located a few steps away, created to provide shelter for pilgrims and assistance to the poor.

  • The inscription: The scroll you see above the Madonna in the photo served to claim ownership of the property and to remind passers-by who guaranteed the spiritual (and material) protection of that stretch of road.

The Disappeared Canal

Today, Via Avesella appears to be a dry alley, but water still flows beneath the pavement. In the past, life in this area was driven by mills and washerwomen who used the canal. The votive shrine also served as a source of comfort and protection for those who worked in an area that was at the time very humid and subject to frequent flooding.

A Refuge for Artists

After the religious congregations were dissolved by Napoleon, the interior spaces became sculpture workshops. The area between Via Tanari and Via Avesella was for decades the beating heart of 19th-century Bolognese sculpture. Even today, if you look beyond the doorways in that area, you can glimpse courtyards that retain the charm of old art workshops.

The "Mysterious" Niche

The small empty niche on the corner seen in the photo is particularly interesting: these cavities often housed very simple terracotta statuettes, known as "Earthquake Madonnas" or protectors of travellers, which were removed or lost over time.

In Avesella there is another votive shrine

In the immediate vicinity, at number 28 of Via Avesella , there is another votive shrine which instead depicts Saint Anthony with the Child , a testimony to the richness of this type of popular devotion in the neighbourhood.
