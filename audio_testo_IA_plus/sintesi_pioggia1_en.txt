**Narrator:** The mystery of the staircase. Ludovico Mattioli and the "fake" Carracci.

Imagine crossing the threshold of the Sanctuary of the Madonna della Pioggia, at the corner of Via Riva di Reno and Via Galliera. Many enter to admire the altar, but the real treasure for the eyes is hidden along the staircase leading to the upper rooms.

There, an artwork awaits you that for a long time deceived even the experts: the evocative landscape with Saint Bartholomew. For years, it was whispered to be the work of the legendary Annibale Carracci, the pioneer of the ideal landscape. But art history is made of discoveries. Today we know that the brush belonged to his brilliant successor: Ludovico Mattioli.

We are in the Bolognese eighteenth century, an era of great lightness and light. Mattioli does not seek the heroic and solemn nature of the past; he prefers a more airy, almost vibrant art, where the atmosphere and lighting effects seem to almost move before your eyes.

Observing the work, you will notice how the landscape is not just a background, but the true protagonist. In the midst of this picturesque and free nature, the figure of Saint Bartholomew appears. It is a profound homage to the ancient soul of this place, which, before becoming the Sanctuary of the Pioggia, was the Oratory of San Bartolomeo di Reno, with its adjoining hospice.

Mattioli's work blends perfectly with the eighteenth-century reconstruction of the church by Alfonso Torreggiani: it is a puzzle piece that completes the baroque harmony of this building.

So, the next time you enter Via Riva di Reno 124, don't just stick to the main navy. Look for the staircase, stop in front of that landscape, and let yourself be captured by Mattioli's light. It is the perfect reminder that in Bologna, sometimes, you only need to climb a few steps to discover that beauty is not always where everyone looks for it. It is a short journey through time, into the heart of the Bolognese 1700s.
