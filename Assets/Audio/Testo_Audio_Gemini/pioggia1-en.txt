Welcome to the corner of via Riva di Reno and via Galliera... right here stands the Sanctuary of the Madonna della Pioggia and San Bartolomeo di Reno. 

This sacred place holds fascinating stories, layered over the centuries. 

If you walk inside and head towards the staircase leading to the upper rooms of the church, you will find yourself in front of a very special work of art... it is the Landscape with Saint Bartholomew. 

For a long time, local tradition mistakenly attributed this canvas to the famous Annibale Carracci, the master who revolutionized landscape painting in the seventeenth century. 

In reality, later studies revealed a different, but equally fascinating truth. 

The painting is the work of a great successor of the Bolognese school... the painter and engraver Ludovico Mattioli, who lived between the late seventeenth and mid-eighteenth centuries. 

As you look at the canvas, you will notice how the style is quite different from the heroic solemnity of the Carracci family. 

Mattioli painted in the heart of the eighteenth century, an era in which landscape had become an independent genre, light and airy, influenced by late-baroque and rococo tastes. 

Nature here is vibrant, less structured, defined by soft brushstrokes and a preference for atmospheric light effects. 

The scene shows Saint Bartholomew, the martyred apostle, immersed in a lush natural setting. This figure is not accidental... it serves as a deep historical link to the ancient dedication of the church and the nearby hospice, which were once reference points for pilgrims and the needy. 

The entire building around you has undergone profound transformations. 

The structure you admire today dates mainly from the eighteenth century, rebuilt based on an elegant project attributed to the architect Alfonso Torreggiani. 

Mattioli's work, positioned along the staircase, fits perfectly into this refined decorative context. 

We invite you to pause for a few moments on the details of the trees, the reflections of the water, and the softness of the luminous tones, letting yourself be carried away by the slow and peaceful rhythm of this eighteenth-century Bologna.
